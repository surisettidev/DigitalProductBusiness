## Section 1 — Do you actually need GST registration?

### The problem you're solving

Most freelancers register for GST too early, pay GST on every invoice, then file empty returns every month. That's wasted time and wasted money.

Some freelancers don't register when they should, and get hit with a ₹10,000+ penalty plus back-taxes when caught. That's worse.

The rule is simple. The application of it is not.

### The rules, exactly

- **Aggregate turnover threshold:** GST registration is mandatory when your **aggregate turnover** (all supplies, taxable + exempt + export) crosses **₹20 lakh in a financial year** (1 April to 31 March).
- **Special category states (NE + hill states):** threshold is **₹10 lakh**. If you are in Manipur, Mizoram, Nagaland, Tripura, Meghalaya, Sikkim, Arunachal Pradesh, Himachal Pradesh, Uttarakhand, or the former state of Jammu & Kashmir (UT of J&K and Ladakh), use the lower limit.
- **Inter-state supply rule:** even if your turnover is below ₹20L, the moment you supply to a client in a different state, GST registration is mandatory. There is no threshold exemption for inter-state.
- **Export of services rule:** zero-rated, but you still need registration to claim the refund of input GST. So register even for export-only.
- **Casual taxable person / non-resident:** registration is mandatory regardless of threshold.

**Verdict: Use the ₹20L threshold only if you are 100% intra-state, 100% domestic, and you measure turnover correctly.** Otherwise register. (Verify before publication: thresholds for FY 2026-27.)

### The "aggregate turnover" trap

Aggregate turnover includes:
- Taxable supplies (your freelance invoices)
- Exempt supplies (rare for freelancers, but some training/coaching falls here)
- Export of services (your USD invoices)
- Supplies made by your GSTIN on behalf of others (rare for solo freelancers)

It does **not** include:
- Inward supplies on which you pay RCM (reverse charge) — those are on the input side
- Pure capital contributions / loans

**What I'd actually use:**

- **You earn < ₹20L, all domestic, all intra-state:** Skip GST. File nil. Save ₹2,000-₹5,000/year in CA fees for nil returns.
- **You earn ₹20L-₹50L:** Register. Use the Composition Scheme at 6% (services, no input credit) **only** if you don't bill international clients and don'thave heavy input GST (laptop, software). Switch to regular at 18% with input credit the month you cross ₹50L.
- **You earn > ₹50L:** Regular scheme only. Pay 18% on services, claim input credit, file GSTR-1 and GSTR-3B monthly.
- **You bill only international clients (export of services):** Register on day one. Export is zero-rated, so you collect 0% GST, but registration is required to claim the refund of GST you paid on inputs (laptop, software, internet).

### Bottom-line ₹ / hour savings

Direct savings: ₹15,000-₹25,000/year by skipping CA fees for monthly nil returns when you're under threshold and intra-state. Add another ₹30,000-₹60,000/year by not over-paying GST in the Composition Scheme when you should be in regular (most freelancers exit Composition in year 2 and get stuck with unclaimed input credit — read Section 2 carefully).

## Section 2 — GST scheme choice: Composition vs Regular

### The problem you're solving

You have registered for GST. Now you have to pick: Composition Scheme (simpler, but you can't claim input credit) or Regular (more paperwork, but you can claim input credit back). Most freelancers pick wrong and lose ₹30K-₹80K/year.

### Composition Scheme (services)

- **Tax rate:** 6% of turnover (effective, 5% CGST + 5% SGST for intra-state, no IGST — actually 6% total for services under Section 10).
- **No input credit allowed.** Whatever GST you paid on laptop, SaaS, software, co-working is sunk.
- **Cannot make inter-state supplies.** Zero international export. Zero interstate B2B.
- **Cannot use "tax invoice" — issue "bill of supply" instead.** Some clients (B2B, mid-size companies) refuse bill of supply. Lost business.
- **Annual turnover cap:** ₹50 lakh for services (revised; verify for 2026-27).
- **No TDS credit for you.** Some clients deduct TDS under Section 194J — you can claim it in ITR, not in GST.

**Verdict: Use only if you are 100% intra-state, 100% domestic, under ₹50L, and have minimal input GST (under ₹2,000/month).**

### Regular Scheme

- **Tax rate:** 18% on services (9% CGST + 9% SGST intra-state; 18% IGST inter-state; 0% on exports with LUT).
- **Input credit allowed** on GST paid for: laptop, software (SaaS), internet, mobile bill (partial), co-working, training/courses taken for work, accountant fees, even rent if you have a registered office.
- **Monthly returns:** GSTR-1 (outward supplies, 11th of next month) and GSTR-3B (summary + payment, 20th of next month). If turnover < ₹5 crore, you can opt for **QRMP** (quarterly return, monthly payment) — saves ~8 hours/month.
- **Annual return:** GSTR-9 (only if turnover > ₹2 crore; verify for 2026-27).
- **LUT for exporters:** Letter of Undertaking on the GST portal. Lets you export at 0% without paying IGST. File it once, valid for 1 year, auto-renewable.

**Verdict: Use for everyone else. The 12% extra you "pay" is offset by input credit on tools you already buy.**

### What I'd actually use

- **Solo freelancer, intra-state, < ₹50L, mostly GA/Photoshop/hosting tools, no exports:** Composition. Honest answer.
- **Solo freelancer, anything else:** Regular. Most freelancers are here.
- **Agency of 2-5 freelancers:** Regular. Always. (Composition caps kill you as soon as you cross ₹50L and you cannot smoothly transition — you lose unclaimed input credit on the laptop you bought in April.)
- **Anyone billing overseas:** Regular, with LUT. Mandatory.

### Bottom-line ₹ / hour savings

Right scheme choice = **₹30,000-₹80,000/year** in either direction. Wrong scheme = lost input credit on ₹2L-₹5L of annual SaaS/tool spend.

A worked example: if you spend ₹3,000/month on SaaS (Zoho, Notion, Adobe, AWS) all carrying 18% GST, that's ₹32,400/year in input GST. Composition forfeits it. Regular claims it. The difference is ₹32,400/year = a Tool Stack purchase every 6 months.

## Section 3 — Section 44ADA: presumptive taxation explained with real numbers

### The problem you're solving

44ADA is the one tax provision built for freelancers. Most freelancers either don't use it (overpay tax by 30-50%) or claim it without keeping the right books (get a notice in 3 years). This section gives you the exact rule and the worked examples.

### What 44ADA actually says

- **Who can use it:** resident individual, HUF, or partnership firm (not LLP) engaged in a specified profession.
- **Specified professions (Section 44AA(1)):** legal, medical, engineering, architecture, accountancy, technical consultancy, interior decoration, or any other profession as notified.
- **"Profession" is interpreted broadly** — IT freelancing, design, writing, marketing consulting all qualify. The CBDT has not formally notified "freelance software developer" but ITAT rulings consistently allow it. **Verify before publication: latest ITAT rulings for 2025-26.**
- **Turnover cap:** gross receipts must be **≤ ₹75 lakh** (raised from ₹50L; verify for 2026-27).
- **Cash receipts cap:** cannot exceed **5% of total turnover**. If you take more than 5% in cash, you forfeit 44ADA for that year.
- **Presumptive income:** **50% of gross receipts** is deemed your business income. You don't need to maintain books. You can claim the other 50% as expenses without proof.
- **Advance tax:** payable in 4 installments — 15% by 15 June, 45% by 15 Sept, 75% by 15 Dec, 100% by 15 March. Skip this and pay 1% monthly interest under Sections 234B and 234C.

### Three worked examples

**Example A: Ramesh, ₹24L/year freelancer**

- Gross receipts FY 2026-27: ₹24,00,000
- 50% deemed income: ₹12,00,000
- Standard deduction under 44ADA: ₹12,00,000 (no books required)
- Taxable income (assume no other income): ₹12,00,000
- Tax under new regime FY 2026-27 (verify slabs): roughly ₹95,000 + cess = ₹98,800
- Effective tax rate: **4.1% of gross receipts**

**Verdict: Use 44ADA. The ₹12L "deemed" deduction saves Ramesh ~₹1.5L/year vs. trying to prove actual expenses.**

**Example B: Priya, ₹10L/year part-time freelancer**

- Gross receipts: ₹10,00,000
- 50% deemed income: ₹5,00,000
- Tax (new regime): roughly ₹25,000 + cess
- Effective rate: **2.5% of gross receipts**

**Verdict: Use 44ADA. The deduction is generous, and you don't need to maintain books.**

**Example C: Aman, ₹50L/year, agency of 2**

- Gross receipts: ₹50,00,000
- 50% deemed income: ₹25,00,000
- Tax (new regime): roughly ₹5,00,000 + cess
- Effective rate: **10% of gross receipts**

**Verdict: At ₹50L, consider exiting 44ADA.** If Aman's actual expenses (salary to the second freelancer, software, rent, equipment) exceed ₹25L, the regular books-of-accounts route will save tax. Most agencies with 2+ people should be in regular regime, not 44ADA.

### What I'd actually use

- **You earn < ₹50L, you don't have heavy documented expenses:** 44ADA. Clean. Simple. Less audit risk.
- **You earn ₹50L-₹75L with real equipment/salary costs:** run the math both ways. Most freelancers with salary expenses > 30% of revenue should exit 44ADA.
- **You earn > ₹75L:** 44ADA does not apply. You must maintain books. Most freelancers in this band need a CA.

### Bottom-line ₹ / hour savings

Right 44ADA choice = **₹30,000-₹2,00,000/year** in tax differential. For a ₹24L freelancer like Ramesh, the difference between 44ADA and "I will just show all my bank credits as income" is ~₹1.5L/year.

## Section 4 — TDS: when clients deduct, how to claim it back

### The problem you're solving

Your client paid you ₹1,00,000 and deposited ₹90,090 in your bank. The ₹9,910 is "TDS" they kept. You need to claim it. Most freelancers don't, and lose that money forever. This section gives you the exact form and process.

### When TDS applies to your freelance income

- **Section 194J (professional fees):** 10% TDS if your gross exceeds **₹30,000 in a year from one client** (verify for 2026-27; the ₹30K threshold was raised in some interpretations). Applies to professional/technical services.
- **Section 194C (contractor):** 1% (individual) / 2% (others) on contract payments. Most "freelance" work is 194J, not 194C, but if you have a long-term retainer with deliverables (not pure advice/consulting), 194C may apply. **Verify classification with a CA.**
- **Section 195 (foreign remittance):** applies when an Indian client pays a non-resident. Doesn't apply to you if you're a resident freelancer.
- **No TDS threshold issue:** if gross from one client < ₹30K and it's 194J, technically no TDS. But the client may still deduct. Ask them to issue a lower deduction / nil deduction certificate (Section 197) if you can show your total income is below the basic exemption.

### How to claim TDS back

1. **Verify TDS deposited by client:** check Form 26Q / 26QAA statement on the TDS Traces portal (https://www.tdscpc.gov.in) using your PAN. It appears within 7-15 days of the client's quarterly filing.
2. **Match with your bank credits:** cross-verify the amount and quarter.
3. **Include in ITR:** when you file ITR-3 or ITR-4 (depending on regime), declare the gross receipt and the TDS credit. AIS (Annual Information Statement) auto-populates the TDS. Verify against your records.
4. **Refund:** if your total TDS + advance tax > final tax liability, refund processes within 3-6 months of ITR filing. Interest at 6% per annum (Section 244A) on refund > ₹100.

**Verdict: Use AIS / 26AS. Don't trust the client's word that "TDS deducted hai, adjust kar lena" without verifying.**

### What I'd actually use

- **One big client deducting TDS:** every quarter, log into TDS Traces, verify deposit, save the PDF. Takes 10 minutes. Saves ₹10,000-₹50,000 in TDS that "disappeared" in the past.
- **Many small clients deducting TDS:** same process, just more lines. Use the AIS auto-populate — don't hand-type.
- **You want to avoid TDS entirely:** ask long-term clients to pay you a lower fee net of TDS + reimbursing the TDS (effectively a 10% rate hike). Most will agree if the alternative is dealing with TDS deposit themselves.

### Bottom-line ₹ / hour savings

TDS reconciliation: 2-4 hours/year, **₹10,000-₹1,00,000+ recovered** depending on your client mix. Many freelancers leave 5-15% of TDS unclaimed over 3-4 years. That's real money left in the income tax department's pocket.

## Section 5 — Foreign payments: Wise, Payoneer, and direct bank

### The problem you're solving

You bill an overseas client in USD. The money lands in your Indian bank. You have no idea if that's taxable, when, and whether you need an FIRC, an LUT, or both. This is the most-messed-up area in Indian freelance taxation.

### Three payment routes compared

| Route | Fee | Speed to INR | FIRC available? | FEMA issue |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Direct bank wire (SWIFT)** | ₹500-₹2,000 + bank spread | 2-5 days | Yes, ask bank | None |
| **Wise (formerly TransferWise)** | 0.5-1.5% + small flat | 1-3 days | Wise issues an "FIRC-equivalent" PDF on request for some corridors | None if routed through their Indian partner |
| **Payoneer** | 0-2% (depends on currency) | 1-3 days | Payoneer issues FIRC for Indian payouts to Indian bank | None if you receive in INR directly |

**Verdict: Use Wise for the cheapest rate, but verify they issue a FIRC-equivalent for your corridor. Use direct bank wire for the cleanest FIRC. Avoid PayPal — fees and FIRC headaches are brutal for India.**

### What is FIRC and why you need it

FIRC = Foreign Inward Remittance Certificate. It proves the money came from overseas, supports your claim of "export of services," and is the document the GST officer and income tax officer both want to see during scrutiny.

- **FIRC issued by your bank** for direct SWIFT wires — ask for "FIRC" in writing at the branch. Takes 3-7 days.
- **FIRC-equivalent from Wise** — Wise India (RBI-licensed) issues a "foreign inward remittance advice" for payments routed through their Indian entity. Save the PDF.
- **FIRC from Payoneer** — Payoneer issues a "Payment Proof" document for INR payouts. Some banks accept it, some don't. Verify with your bank.

**What I'd actually use:**

- **You bill 1-2 big overseas clients in USD/EUR/GBP:** open a Wise multi-currency account, route all receipts there, then convert to INR and transfer to your Indian bank weekly or monthly. Use Wise's FIRC-equivalent for your ITR / GST audit files. Total cost: 0.7-1.2% per transfer.
- **You bill 10+ small clients via Upwork/Toptal/Fiverr:** these platforms already handle FIRC. Download the "Tax Invoice" PDF from the platform dashboard every quarter. Use it for your ITR.
- **You get paid in crypto:** do not. RBI PMLA issues aside, banks flag crypto-linked deposits, and the tax treatment is murky. (Verify before publication: 2026-27 crypto-tax rules.)

### FEMA, LRS, and the export-of-services angle

- **FEMA (Foreign Exchange Management Act):** the RBI rulebook for foreign currency. Export of services is governed by the Foreign Exchange Management (Export of Goods and Services) Regulations. As a freelancer exporting services, you must receive the full foreign currency within **9 months** (verify for 2026-27) of the invoice date. After that, the bank can demand you convert the receivable.
- **LRS (Liberalised Remittance Scheme):** applies to *outward* remittance (you paying someone abroad). Most freelancers don't need LRS — they receive money, not send it. Exception: you pay a foreign SaaS tool (Ahrefs, ConvertKit, etc.) — that's LRS, ₹2,50,000/year cap, documentation required. Tax is not the issue; the LRS cap is.
- **Export of services = zero-rated GST (with LUT).** You charge 0% to your overseas client, pay 0% GST, but claim refund of input GST you paid (laptop, software, etc.). Without LUT, you pay 18% IGST on every export invoice and then claim refund — which is a 3-6 month cash flow hit.

**Verdict: File the LUT on day one of GST registration if you bill even one overseas client. Don't wait for the first USD invoice.**

### Bottom-line ₹ / hour savings

Right payment routing: **₹10,000-₹50,000/year** in fees + **₹30,000-₹1,00,000/year** in cash-flow improvement by filing LUT (no IGST lockup). Plus: **₹50,000-₹3,00,000/year** in input GST refund you would have missed without the right documentation.

## Section 6 — Advance tax: the schedule and the cheap mistake to avoid

### The problem you're solving

"Advance tax" is a confusing name. It's not a tax on income you've earned in advance. It's a quarterly installment of your final year's tax liability. Skip it, and you pay 1% monthly interest — that's 12% annualized for what is essentially a free loan to the government.

### The schedule

| Installment | Due date | % of full-year estimated tax |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | 15 June | 15% |
| 2nd | 15 September | 45% |
| 3rd | 15 December | 75% |
| 4th | 15 March | 100% |

**Important nuance:** if your total tax liability for the year is **< ₹10,000** after TDS, you don't have to pay advance tax. Most freelancers earning < ₹5L don't. Verify this limit for 2026-27.

**Verdict: Pay advance tax if your projected full-year tax > ₹10,000.** Use the calculator in Appendix B.

### What I'd actually use

- **You earn < ₹5L/year and don't expect tax > ₹10K:** skip advance tax. Pay it all by 31 March as "self-assessment tax." No penalty.
- **You earn ₹5L-₹20L:** pay advance tax quarterly. The 1% monthly interest on missed installments is wasted money. A ₹50K tax liability in default costs ₹6,000/year in interest.
- **You earn > ₹20L:** pay advance tax religiously, plus put a reminder in your calendar for the 15th of each quarter. Most freelancers miss the September installment because they're heads-down on client work.
- **TDS already covers your full liability:** verify using 26AS. If your TDS + advance tax ≥ final tax, no extra payment needed. Many full-time freelancers with one salaried + one freelance income are in this bucket.

### Bottom-line ₹ / hour savings

Avoiding 234B/234C interest: **₹2,000-₹30,000/year** depending on income. At 12% annualized on a ₹1L advance tax default, that's ₹12,000/year — a new laptop bag.

## Section 7 — The minimal stack: what you need vs what is overkill

### The problem you're solving

You don't need a CA for everything. You don't need ClearTax/Zoho for everything. You don't need a lawyer. This section gives you the tier-0 / tier-1 / tier-2 stack.

### Tier 0 (₹0/month, you do it yourself)

- **Invoicing:** Zoho Invoice (free for 5 clients) or a GST-compliant Word/Google Docs template.
- **Bookkeeping:** Google Sheet — one row per invoice, one row per expense. Tally at the end of the month.
- **GST filing:** gst.gov.in direct portal. Takes 30 min/month for GSTR-1, 30 min/month for GSTR-3B.
- **TDS reconciliation:** tdscpc.gov.in portal, 2 hours/year.
- **ITR filing:** incometaxindia.gov.in e-filing portal. ITR-4 if 44ADA, ITR-3 if regular. Use ClearTax free tier for 1 year for the first time; then DIY.

**Verdict: Use this for the first ₹10L/year. Move to Tier 1 at ₹10L+, Tier 2 at ₹30L+.**

### Tier 1 (₹500-₹2,000/month, you do it, software helps)

- **Invoicing:** Refrens (India-made, ₹249/month) or Zoho Invoice Plus. Auto-generates GST invoice, sends payment reminders, tracks receipts.
- **Bookkeeping:** Zoho Books (₹749/month for the Indian edition). Reconciles bank feed, generates P&L, files GST.
- **CA on retainer:** ₹1,000-₹2,000/month for ITR + quarterly review. Most freelancers only need CA for ITR + scrutiny response, not monthly.

### Tier 2 (₹5,000-₹15,000/month, you pay a CA, they do GST + ITR + TDS)

- **Full CA retainer:** ₹5,000-₹15,000/month for monthly GST + TDS filing + quarterly review + annual ITR. Worth it at ₹30L+ revenue or any time you have international clients.
- **Chartered accountant firm:** Tally-integrated, multi-CA, you get a junior assigned. ₹10,000-₹20,000/month.

### Bottom-line ₹ / hour savings

Tier 0 vs Tier 2 differential: **₹60,000-₹1,80,000/year** in fees. Most freelancers who jump to Tier 1 too early pay for software they don't need. Stay at Tier 0 until ₹10L. Stay at Tier 1 until ₹30L. Then go to Tier 2.

## Section 8 — The CA meeting cheat sheet

### The problem you're solving

You book a CA meeting. You pay ₹2,000-₹5,000 for an hour. You walk in unprepared, they ask generic questions, you leave with ₹3,000 of advice and a ₹15,000 invoice. Most freelancers do this 2-3 times a year. This section gives you the cheat sheet.

### What to bring to your CA (one-page checklist)

- [ ] PAN card + Aadhaar (linked)
- [ ] GST registration certificate (if registered)
- [ ] Bank statement — all accounts — full year (PDF)
- [ ] Form 26AS / AIS (download from incometaxindia.gov.in)
- [ ] List of all invoices issued (with GSTIN if B2B)
- [ ] List of all expenses (grouped: software, equipment, internet, travel, rent, salary)
- [ ] Foreign client list + FIRC PDFs (if any)
- [ ] TDS deposit details (challan copies)
- [ ] Previous year ITR acknowledgment
- [ ] Any notices received (scrutiny, GST mismatch)
- [ ] Specific questions (write them down — don't wing it)

### Questions to ask your CA

1. "Given my revenue mix, am I on the right GST scheme?"
2. "Should I exit 44ADA this year? Run the math both ways."
3. "Did I miss claiming any TDS credit in the last 3 years?"
4. "Is my LUT filed? When does it expire?"
5. "What is my tax-saving headroom for the next 3 months?" (80C, 80D, NPS)
6. "Any mismatch in my GST returns that I should disclose before they turn into a notice?"
7. "If I take a foreign client worth ₹X, what's the cleanest way to receive it?"

**Verdict: Use this checklist. Your CA meeting will go from vague-advice to specific-action, and you'll save 30-50% of the billable hours.**

### Bottom-line ₹ / hour savings

Prepared CA meeting: **₹3,000-₹8,000 saved per meeting** by cutting wasted time, plus **₹20,000-₹1,00,000/year in identified tax savings** (unclaimed TDS, wrong scheme, missed deductions).

- **Verify before publication:** HSN/SAC code 998314 (IT services) — IT and software services are under 9983xx series; specific code may have been revised. Cross-check cbic-gst.gov.in HSN master.
